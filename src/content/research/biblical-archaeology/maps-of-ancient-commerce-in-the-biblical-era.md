### Biblical References
 
The Bible depicts various trade activities in its narratives, painting a vivid picture of the era's commerce. Intriguing stories, like that of the Queen of Sheba's visit to King Solomon, not only highlight diplomatic exchanges but also the robust trade relations between distant lands.
 
In Ezekiel 27, the text describes the city of Tyre as a hub of commerce, likening it to a marketplace for the nations. This passage provides substantial insights into the diversity of trade partners and goods, from precious metals to textiles and spices, that moved through such centers.
 
### Historical Background
 
The regions mentioned in Biblical texts were part of a larger network that spanned across the ancient Near East and Mediterranean. These areas, rich in natural resources and strategic positions, were vital to the trade routes connecting Asia, Africa, and Europe. The exchange of goods like gold, incense, myrrh, and various textiles was commonplace, contributing to the wealth and cultural exchange of the region.
 
### Geographic Location
 
The epicenter of Biblical commerce stretched from the Levant to the fertile crescent, encompassing todays Middle East. The Mediterranean Sea served as a crucial corridor for maritime trade, facilitating the exchange of goods between Europe, Asia, and Africa. Key land routes ran through regions like Canaan, offering connections to the wider world.
 
### Recent Findings
 
Archaeological excavations continually illuminate our understanding of commerce during this era. Recent discoveries include ancient ports like the one found at Tell Abu Hawam, identified as a Phoenician trade hub. Findings such as tablets, seals, and trade records further affirm the existence and complexity of these ancient networks.
 
### Archaeological Evidence
 
The physical remnants of ancient commerce offer tantalizing clues to the era's trade dynamics. Discoveries of specific artifacts and implements used in trade allow scholars to piece together the daily activities and broader economic structures at play.
 
### Specific Artifacts
 
Artifacts such as scales, weights, and ancient coinage serve as tangible proof of vigorous trade activities. These items not only affirm the practice of trade but also the sophistication and precision involved in transactions during the era. Other significant finds include storage jars and amphorae that were used to transport goods like wine and olive oil across vast distances.
 
### Dating Methods
 
Accurate dating methods, such as carbon dating, as well as stylistic analyses of pottery and inscriptions, allow archaeologists to place these artifacts within a specific timeframe. This, in turn, helps construct a more precise narrative of trade practices during the Biblical era.
 
### Physical Descriptions
