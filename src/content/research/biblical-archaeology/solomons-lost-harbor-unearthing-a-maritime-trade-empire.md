## Discovery Context
 
### Biblical Reference and Historical Background
 
In biblical texts, King Solomon is celebrated not only for his wisdom and wealth but also for his flourishing trade empire. His era, marked by prosperity and grandeur, witnessed remarkable construction projects, including the Temple in Jerusalem. He is renowned for establishing alliances that extended his influence across the ancient world. The biblical narrative in the Book of Kings describes Solomons ventures, mentioning fleets that sailed to Ophir and Tarshish, bringing back gold, silver, ivory, apes, and peacocks, hinting at his extensive marine enterprises.
 
### Geographic Location and Recent Findings
 
The suspected location of Solomon's harbor aligns geographically with regions within the ancient kingdom of Edom, corresponding to modern-day Aqaba in Jordan. Recent archaeological activities have unearthed remnants of a harbor that scholars believe might be part of this illustrious network. These findings emerged from extensive excavations revealing ancient docks, warehouses, and a plethora of artifacts bespeaking a lively trading hub. The conjunction of these geographical and archaeological cues strengthens the hypothesis of its association with King Solomon's famed fleets.
 
## Archaeological Evidence
 
### Artifacts and Their Significance
 
Excavations have brought to light numerous artifacts, including Phoenician inscriptions, pottery shards, and remnants of wooden structures that suggest a combination of local and foreign craftsmanship. These artifacts offer tangible proof of the regions active involvement in trade, echoing the historical recollections of biblical narratives. They reflect not only the trade of valuable commodities but also the cultural interchanges that such commerce facilitated.
 
### Dating Methods and Expert Interpretations
 
Archaeological teams employed various dating methods including radiocarbon dating and dendrochronology, which place these findings within the timeline coinciding with King Solomon's reign, approximately the 10th century BCE. Experts interpret these physical remnants as affirmations of interconnected trade routes stretching as far as the ancient Indus and Egyptian civilizations. As experts like Dr. Sara Hamilton from the University of Jerusalem argue, these discoveries lend considerable weight to the historical accounts of Solomon's extensive maritime prowess.
