## History of Bible Translations

Throughout the centuries, the history of Bible translations has been characterized by a continuous evolution to cater to diverse linguistic communities. The Bible, as a sacred text, holds profound significance for millions worldwide. Its original manuscripts were written in Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek, with the Old Scripture primarily in Hebrew and Aramaic, and the New Scripture in Greek. The need for translations arose as communities sought to understand the Word of God in their native languages, leading to the emergence of various versions over time.

Early translations of the Bible were mostly in Latin, with St. Jerome's Vulgate becoming a pivotal version in the 4th century. The Gutenberg Bible, printed in the 15th century, revolutionized the dissemination of biblical texts, enabling mass production and wider access. In 1611, the King James Version (KJV) was published, becoming one of the most influential English translations. Its majestic language and poetic style shaped subsequent versions and influenced English literature.

As the practice of Bible study evolved, modern translations such as the NIV, ESV, and NLT emerged to strike a balance between accuracy and readability for contemporary audiences. These translations aim to make the Word of God accessible to a broader range of readers while remaining faithful to the original texts. The history of Bible translations reflects a continual effort to convey the message of the Bible across cultures and languages, enriching the spiritual lives of believers worldwide.

## Controversies Over Mistranslations

Conservative groups leveraged this mistranslation to advance an anti-gay agenda, causing substantial harm.

However, in 1971, a revision based on historical context led to a change in translation from 'homosexual' to 'sexual perverts,' aiming to rectify the misleading interpretation.
